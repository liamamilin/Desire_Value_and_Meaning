6  What I Care About

Care reveals value.

A person may not always know what they value by abstract reflection. They may say many things are important: truth, freedom, love, achievement, health, family, dignity, creativity, responsibility, peace, beauty, money, recognition, or meaning.

But claimed values are not always reliable.

A person may claim to value health while destroying the body.
Claim to value truth while avoiding honest speech.
Claim to value freedom while surrendering agency.
Claim to value love while refusing repair.
Claim to value serious work while chasing only appearance.
Claim to value dignity while accepting degradation for approval.

Care is different.

Care appears where the self is emotionally, practically, and morally invested.

Care says:

This matters to me.

I cannot treat this as nothing.

I feel responsible toward this.

I feel pain when this is damaged.

I feel called to protect this.

I return to this even when reward is absent.

This chapter begins from a central claim:

Care is the lived evidence of value: it reveals what the self treats as important through attention, attachment, responsibility, protection, grief, anger, and commitment.

Care makes value visible.

Not only in words.

But in what the self protects, returns to, suffers for, repairs, refuses to abandon, and cannot betray without losing self-respect.

6.1 Care as Evidence of Value

Care shows where value has entered the self.

A person may not be able to define their core values clearly, but they can observe care.

They can ask:

What do I keep thinking about?

What do I protect?

What hurts when it is damaged?

What makes me angry when it is disrespected?

What do I feel responsible for?

What do I return to even after discouragement?

What would I regret abandoning?

What do I still care about when no one rewards me?

These questions reveal value more honestly than slogans.

A person may say they value money, but what they care about may be freedom and safety.

They may say they value success, but what they care about may be dignity and recognition.

They may say they value peace, but what they care about may be recovery and emotional safety.

They may say they value achievement, but what they care about may be mastery, contribution, or proof against shame.

Care points beneath the value word.

It shows the living attachment.

6.2 Care Is Not Mere Interest

Interest is lighter than care.

Interest says:

This attracts my attention.
This is enjoyable.
This is intellectually or emotionally engaging.

Care says:

This matters enough that I feel invested in its condition, future, or integrity.

A person may be interested in many things.

They may be interested in history, technology, psychology, art, politics, business, language, travel, philosophy, or other people’s lives.

But care is deeper.

Care has weight.

When the cared-for thing is neglected, damaged, misunderstood, or threatened, the self responds.

For example:

Interest:
I like reading about education.

Care:
I feel responsible for how people learn, how knowledge is structured, and how learning can change a life.
Interest:
I enjoy language.

Care:
I cannot treat language as trivial because I see it as the carrier of thought, identity, and action.
Interest:
I like systems.

Care:
I feel that unclear systems produce confusion, waste, and weak action; I want to make structure visible.

Interest may become care when it gains responsibility, meaning, and persistence.

The self should ask:

Is this only something I enjoy?

Or is this something I feel called to protect, develop, repair, or understand deeply?

6.3 Attention Reveals Care

Care often appears as returning attention.

The self returns to what matters.

It may return in thought.
Return in worry.
Return in imagination.
Return in planning.
Return in memory.
Return in work.
Return in conversation.
Return in anger.
Return in grief.
Return in repeated attempts.

A person may care about something if attention returns even when the immediate reward is weak.

They keep thinking about a project.
Keep returning to a question.
Keep noticing a problem.
Keep imagining a future.
Keep feeling pain around an unresolved value.
Keep wanting to repair a relationship.
Keep studying a field.
Keep building a language for something that matters.

The self should ask:

Where does my attention return?

What question keeps coming back?

What problem do I keep seeing?

What future keeps appearing?

What wound keeps asking to be understood?

What work keeps calling me even when I leave it?

Attention is not always care.

Attention can also be anxiety, obsession, addiction, or fear.

But repeated attention is evidence.

It should be examined.

6.4 Care and Attachment

Care involves attachment.

The self is not neutral toward what it cares about.

It is attached to its existence, quality, future, dignity, truth, or development.

Attachment may appear toward:

a person
a family
a body
a project
a craft
a field
a language
a community
a place
a future self
a moral principle
a form of work
a possibility
a dream

Attachment says:

This is not interchangeable.

This has meaning to me.

Its condition affects me.

I cannot replace it with any random object.

This is why care creates vulnerability.

If the self cares, it can be hurt.

If the person cares about love, rejection hurts.
If they care about work, failure hurts.
If they care about truth, deception hurts.
If they care about dignity, humiliation hurts.
If they care about freedom, control hurts.
If they care about health, bodily breakdown hurts.
If they care about meaning, empty activity hurts.

Care opens the self to pain.

But it also opens the self to meaning.

A life without care may be safer in one sense, but it becomes thin.

6.5 Care and Pain

Pain often reveals care.

A person feels pain when something they care about is threatened, lost, ignored, betrayed, or degraded.

For example:

Pain around failure may reveal care about competence, dignity, or future possibility.

Pain around rejection may reveal care about love, belonging, or recognition.

Pain around meaningless work may reveal care about contribution, freedom, or inner coherence.

Pain around being misunderstood may reveal care about truth and accurate recognition.

Pain around family conflict may reveal care about belonging, loyalty, or responsibility.

Pain around bodily exhaustion may reveal care about health and self-protection.

Pain is not automatically value.

Pain may also come from fear, ego, trauma, or shame.

But pain should be read.

The self should ask:

What value is being hurt here?

What care has been threatened?

What did I lose, or fear losing?

Why does this matter so much?

Is this pain about current value, old wound, or both?

Pain becomes useful when it reveals what matters.

It becomes destructive when it is only repeated without interpretation.

6.6 Care and Anger

Anger can reveal care.

People often become angry when something they care about is violated.

Anger may point to:

injustice
disrespect
boundary violation
dishonesty
carelessness
waste
betrayal
exploitation
humiliation
neglect
falsehood

A person who values truth may become angry at distortion.

A person who values dignity may become angry at humiliation.

A person who values responsibility may become angry at irresponsibility.

A person who values craft may become angry at sloppy work.

A person who values freedom may become angry at control.

A person who values love may become angry at neglect or betrayal.

The self should not obey anger blindly.

Anger can exaggerate, distort, and attack.

But anger should be examined.

The question is:

What care is underneath this anger?

What value do I feel has been violated?

Is the anger proportionate?

What responsible action would protect the value without becoming destructive?

Anger is often care in a defensive form.

It should be translated into boundary, truth, repair, or action.

6.7 Care and Grief

Grief reveals care through loss.

The self grieves what mattered.

A person may grieve a relationship because love mattered.
Grieve a missed opportunity because future possibility mattered.
Grieve lost time because becoming mattered.
Grieve a failed project because creation mattered.
Grieve illness because the body and freedom mattered.
Grieve leaving a place because belonging mattered.
Grieve an old self because identity continuity mattered.

Grief says:

This was not nothing.

This had value.

Its loss changes me.

I must make room for what it meant.

Grief is not weakness.

It is evidence of care.

A person who refuses grief may also refuse to know what mattered.

The self should ask:

What am I grieving?

What value did this loss touch?

What did this person, role, future, place, or possibility mean to me?

What remains worth carrying forward?

What must be released?

Grief can clarify value because it shows what the self cannot lose without consequence.

6.8 Care and Responsibility

Care naturally creates responsibility.

If the self cares about something, it cannot treat it casually.

If the self cares about health, it must take some responsibility for body, rhythm, and recovery.

If the self cares about love, it must take responsibility for honesty, communication, repair, and care.

If the self cares about freedom, it must take responsibility for capacity, money, boundary, and consequence.

If the self cares about work, it must take responsibility for craft, completion, and quality.

If the self cares about truth, it must take responsibility for evidence, clarity, and self-correction.

Care without responsibility becomes sentiment.

The self says:

This matters to me.

But if nothing changes in action, care remains weak.

Responsible care asks:

If this matters, what must I do?

What must I protect?

What must I repair?

What must I stop neglecting?

What standard must I hold?

What sacrifice does this care require?

Care becomes value when it accepts responsibility.

6.9 Care Is Not Control

Care can be distorted into control.

A person may say:

I care about you.
I care about this project.
I care about this outcome.
I care about this family.
I care about this future.

But the care may become possessive, anxious, rigid, or dominating.

Control says:

Because I care, everything must happen my way.

Because I care, I must prevent all risk.

Because I care, I cannot tolerate uncertainty.

Because I care, I will manage everyone involved.

Because I care, I will not allow difference.

This is not mature care.

Mature care protects without owning.

It supports without suffocating.

It takes responsibility without controlling what is not its own.

The self should ask:

Am I caring, or controlling?

What is actually mine to protect?

What belongs to another person?

What uncertainty must I tolerate?

What boundary separates care from possession?

Care must respect reality.

Not everything loved can be controlled.

Not everything valued can be guaranteed.

6.10 Care Is Not Anxiety

Anxiety can imitate care.

The self may think it cares because it worries constantly.

But worry is not the same as care.

Worry says:

What if something goes wrong?

What if I lose this?

What if I fail?

What if they leave?

What if I am not enough?

What if this collapses?

Care says:

This matters.

What responsible action protects or serves it?

Anxiety circles.

Care acts.

Anxiety repeats imagined danger.

Care clarifies responsibility.

Anxiety may exhaust the self without protecting the value.

Care asks what can actually be done.

The self should ask:

Am I caring for this,
or only worrying about it?

What action would serve the value?

What fear am I repeating?

What responsibility is actually mine?

What uncertainty must remain?

Anxiety may indicate that something matters, but it is not the same as caring well.

Care must become action, boundary, repair, or acceptance.

6.11 Care Is Not Obligation Alone

Obligation can look like care.

A person may keep doing something because they feel they must.

They may support family, stay in a role, continue a relationship, complete a task, or carry responsibility.

This may be true care.

But it may also be guilt, fear, or role imprisonment.

Obligation says:

I must do this.

Care says:

This matters to me, and I choose responsibility toward it.

The difference is important.

A person can choose responsibility even when they do not feel warm emotion.

Care is not always soft.

Sometimes care is disciplined.

Sometimes care is duty accepted consciously.

But if obligation contains no ownership, resentment grows.

The self should ask:

Do I care about this?

Or do I only fear guilt?

What value does this responsibility serve?

What part of this responsibility is mine?

What part has been imposed?

Can I choose this consciously?

Or must I renegotiate it?

Care and duty can align.

But duty should be named honestly.

6.12 Care and Priority

Care reveals priority.

A person may claim many values, but care shows what actually gets weight.

The self can ask:

What do I protect first?

What do I neglect first?

What do I sacrifice when pressure rises?

What do I refuse to sacrifice?

What do I make time for?

What do I keep postponing?

What do I return to after failure?

Priority is not what the self says is important.

It is what becomes important when time, energy, attention, and resources are limited.

For example:

If I say I care about health,
but always sacrifice sleep first,
then achievement or urgency may have higher priority.

If I say I care about love,
but never make time for repair,
then pride or avoidance may have higher priority.

If I say I care about freedom,
but never build skill or money,
then comfort or fear may have higher priority.

If I say I care about truth,
but always hide to preserve approval,
then belonging or image may have higher priority.

This does not mean the claimed value is false.

It means the priority structure must be examined.

Care must eventually appear in priority.

6.13 Care and Time

Time reveals care.

A person may say something matters, but if no time is given to it, the value remains weak.

Time is one of the most honest forms of care because time is finite.

The self can ask:

What receives my time?

What receives only my words?

What do I say matters but never schedule?

What do I repeatedly postpone?

What do I give time to even when tired?

What do I make room for when life is crowded?

Time does not tell the whole story.

Some people lack time because of survival pressure, work, caregiving, illness, or constraint.

But even then, time reveals struggle.

A person may care deeply about something and suffer because they cannot give it time.

That suffering is also evidence.

The self should ask:

Does my time reflect my values?

If not, why?

Is the mismatch caused by constraint, fear, confusion, habit, or avoidance?

What small time allocation would honor what I care about?

Care needs time to become real.

6.14 Care and Attention Quality

Not only time, but attention quality reveals care.

A person may spend time with something while giving it low-quality attention.

They may be physically present but mentally absent.

They may work on a project without real respect for craft.

They may speak with someone without listening.

They may study without curiosity.

They may rest while still mentally trapped in work.

Care requires quality of presence.

The self should ask:

When I give time, am I actually present?

Do I give this my best attention?

Do I treat this as disposable?

Do I rush through it?

Do I listen?

Do I revise?

Do I protect the conditions for real attention?

Care is not only allocation.

It is presence.

What the self cares about deserves better than leftover attention forever.

6.15 Care and Protection

Care protects.

If something matters, the self must protect it from damage, neglect, distortion, exploitation, and erosion.

A person may need to protect:

health from overwork

truth from self-deception

love from pride and neglect

freedom from dependency

dignity from humiliation

craft from haste and applause addiction

attention from distraction

meaning from empty busyness

body from exploitation

future self from short-term impulse

Care without protection is fragile.

The self says something matters, but allows it to be repeatedly harmed.

The self should ask:

What do I care about that I am failing to protect?

What is damaging it?

What boundary is needed?

What habit must change?

What environment must be redesigned?

What must I refuse?

Protection is one of the clearest expressions of care.

6.16 Care and Neglect

Neglect reveals value conflict.

A person may neglect what they care about because another force has more control.

They may neglect health for achievement.

Neglect truth for approval.

Neglect love for pride.

Neglect freedom for security.

Neglect creativity for duty.

Neglect rest for guilt.

Neglect body for productivity.

Neglect meaning for reward.

Neglect serious work for distraction.

Neglect future self for present relief.

Neglect does not always mean lack of care.

Sometimes neglect means care is losing to another value, fear, habit, or pressure.

The self should ask:

What do I care about but neglect?

What keeps overpowering it?

What value, fear, or habit is taking priority?

What would it look like to care for it in a small but real way?

This question is often painful.

It reveals where values have not yet become structure.

6.17 Care and Repair

Care repairs.

If something valued is damaged, care moves toward repair.

A person who cares about love repairs relationship after harm.

A person who cares about truth corrects falsehood.

A person who cares about craft revises weak work.

A person who cares about health restores the body after neglect.

A person who cares about responsibility addresses consequences.

A person who cares about dignity repairs self-betrayal.

Repair shows that the value is alive.

The self should ask:

When this value is damaged, do I repair?

Or do I abandon, deny, blame, or hide?

What does repair require?

What apology, correction, revision, boundary, or new practice is needed?

Care is not proven by never failing.

It is proven by returning to repair after failure.

6.18 Care and Commitment

Care deepens through commitment.

A person may care emotionally, but commitment gives care continuity.

Commitment says:

This matters beyond today’s mood.

I will continue to serve this value over time.

I will return after difficulty.

I will not abandon it only because reward is delayed.

Care without commitment may remain sentimental.

Commitment without care may become dry duty.

A mature life joins care and commitment.

For example:

I care about writing.
Commitment turns this care into a writing practice.

I care about health.
Commitment turns this care into rhythm and protection.

I care about love.
Commitment turns this care into communication, repair, and presence.

I care about freedom.
Commitment turns this care into skill, savings, boundary, and self-management.

Commitment gives care a future.

6.19 Care and Meaning

Meaning arises where care connects to action.

An activity becomes meaningful when the self sees why it matters.

Work becomes meaningful when connected to craft, contribution, freedom, or responsibility.

Study becomes meaningful when connected to capability, truth, or future self.

Relationship becomes meaningful when connected to love, care, recognition, and shared life.

Sacrifice becomes meaningful when connected to a value worth protecting.

Practice becomes meaningful when connected to becoming.

Meaning disappears when action is disconnected from care.

A person may be busy but feel empty because they do not care about what the activity serves.

They may be successful but feel hollow because success is disconnected from owned value.

They may be productive but feel dead because productivity has no connection to meaning.

The self should ask:

What care does this action serve?

What value is this connected to?

If I feel meaningless, what care has been disconnected?

What would reconnect this action to something that matters?

Care is the emotional root of meaning.

Without care, meaning cannot remain alive.

6.20 Care and Work

Work reveals care.

A person may care about:

quality
usefulness
truth
beauty
service
craft
completion
learning
recognition
income
freedom
contribution

Different forms of care produce different forms of work.

If the self cares about craft, it revises.

If it cares about usefulness, it asks whether the work serves someone.

If it cares about recognition, it seeks audience and feedback.

If it cares about truth, it resists distortion.

If it cares about freedom, it designs work around agency.

If it cares about money, it studies value exchange and sustainability.

The self should ask:

What do I care about in work?

Do I care about the work itself?

The result?

The recognition?

The money?

The freedom it creates?

The contribution?

The identity it gives me?

The craft?

The field?

The people it serves?

Work becomes meaningful when the right form of care is present.

If the work contains no cared-for element, it may become only survival, habit, or reward.

6.21 Care and Relationships

Relationships reveal care in a direct way.

A person may say they care about someone, but care appears through:

attention
listening
respect
time
truth
repair
protection
boundary
patience
responsibility
mutual recognition

Care is not the same as attachment intensity.

A person may feel intense attachment but not care well.

They may want the other person, need the other person, fear losing the other person, or seek validation from the other person, but still fail to listen, respect, or repair.

Mature relational care asks:

Do I care about this person’s reality?

Do I care about their freedom, dignity, and truth?

Do I care enough to repair harm?

Do I care enough to speak honestly?

Do I care enough to respect boundary?

Do I care enough not to use them as proof of my worth?

Care in relationship must become ethical.

Otherwise it becomes possession, hunger, dependency, or performance.

6.22 Care and the Body

The body is often neglected until it fails.

A person may claim to care about the body, but treat it as a tool.

They use it for productivity, appearance, endurance, pleasure, or performance, but do not care for it as a living condition of the self.

Body care asks:

Do I listen to fatigue?

Do I protect sleep?

Do I respect pain?

Do I give the body movement, food, rest, and medical attention?

Do I use the body as an object for status?

Do I punish the body for not matching an image?

Do I exploit the body for ambition?

The body is not separate from value.

Health supports freedom, work, love, thought, and meaning.

If the self cares about any long-term direction, it must care about the body that carries it.

Body care is not vanity.

It is structural responsibility.

6.23 Care and Future Self

Care can extend toward the future self.

A person may care about who they are becoming.

They may protect the future self from present impulse, avoidance, or self-betrayal.

Future-self care says:

I care about the person I am becoming.

I will not sacrifice that person entirely for present relief.

I will build evidence for that future self.

I will protect conditions that make becoming possible.

This kind of care appears in:

study
saving money
health protection
skill building
boundary
completion
practice
ethical choices
relationship repair
long-term work

The self should ask:

Do I care about my future self?

What am I doing that protects that future?

What am I doing that betrays it?

What small action today would be an act of care toward who I am becoming?

Future-self care turns values into time-extended responsibility.

6.24 Care and Self-Respect

Care connects directly to self-respect.

When the self protects what it cares about, self-respect strengthens.

When the self repeatedly betrays what it cares about, self-respect weakens.

A person may feel self-respect when they say the difficult truth because they care about honesty.

When they protect rest because they care about health.

When they refuse humiliation because they care about dignity.

When they complete work because they care about craft.

When they repair harm because they care about responsibility.

When they leave a false belonging because they care about freedom and truth.

The self should ask:

What do I care about that, if betrayed, damages my self-respect?

What do I care about that, if protected, strengthens my self-respect?

Where have I betrayed care?

Where must I repair?

Self-respect is often the self’s memory of how it has treated what matters.

6.25 Care Can Be Conflicted

A person may care about multiple things that conflict.

They may care about freedom and family.

Health and achievement.

Love and truth.

Security and adventure.

Peace and ambition.

Recognition and privacy.

Dignity and belonging.

Meaningful work and financial stability.

This conflict does not mean care is false.

It means the self is human.

The question becomes:

Which care leads here?

Which care must be protected but not dominate?

What care has been neglected too long?

What care has become excessive?

What sacrifice is required?

What compromise preserves the most important values?

Care must eventually become priority.

Without priority, care remains emotionally scattered.

6.26 Care Can Be Excessive

Care can become excessive when it loses proportion.

A person may care so much about achievement that health disappears.

Care so much about family that selfhood disappears.

Care so much about truth that kindness disappears.

Care so much about freedom that commitment disappears.

Care so much about love that dignity disappears.

Care so much about recognition that craft disappears.

Care so much about safety that life becomes narrow.

The problem is not care itself.

The problem is unbalanced care.

The self should ask:

Has this care become too dominant?

What other values is it consuming?

What boundary does this care need?

What proportion would make this care healthier?

A value system must not only identify care.

It must arrange care.

6.27 Care Can Be Underdeveloped

Some forms of care are weak because they were never trained.

A person may not know how to care for the body.

How to care for relationships.

How to care for money.

How to care for work quality.

How to care for truth.

How to care for future self.

How to care for emotional life.

This does not always mean they do not value these things.

It may mean they lack practice, models, knowledge, or emotional permission.

The self should ask:

What do I value but do not know how to care for?

What care skill is missing?

What model do I need?

What small practice would train this care?

Care is not only feeling.

Care is skill.

A person can learn to care better.

6.28 Care and Skill

To care well, the self needs skill.

Love requires relational skill.
Truth requires language and courage.
Health requires body knowledge and discipline.
Freedom requires planning and self-management.
Work requires craft.
Money requires financial skill.
Family requires boundary and responsibility.
Meaning requires reflection and commitment.

Without skill, care may remain helpless.

The self may care but not know what to do.

For example:

I care about health,
but I do not know how to design a sustainable routine.

I care about love,
but I do not know how to communicate or repair.

I care about freedom,
but I do not know how to build resources.

I care about work,
but I do not know how to finish and improve.

I care about truth,
but I do not know how to speak without cruelty or fear.

Care must become competence.

The self should ask:

What skill does this care require?

What practice would make me capable of caring well?

This turns care into development.

6.29 Care and Evidence

Care should be supported by evidence.

A person may say they care, but evidence asks:

What action proves this care?

What time proves it?

What protection proves it?

What repair proves it?

What sacrifice proves it?

What attention proves it?

What boundary proves it?

Evidence does not need to be grand.

Small evidence matters.

A protected hour can prove care for work.

A difficult conversation can prove care for relationship.

A sleep boundary can prove care for health.

A correction can prove care for truth.

A refusal can prove care for dignity.

A savings habit can prove care for freedom.

A revision cycle can prove care for craft.

Care becomes stronger when it creates evidence.

Without evidence, care remains declared but unbuilt.

6.30 The Care Audit

To study care, use the following audit:

Object of Care:
What do I care about?

Form:
Is this care for a person, value, project, body, future, community, truth, or identity?

Evidence:
What proves that I care?

Attention:
How often does my attention return to it?

Emotion:
What pain, anger, grief, joy, or tenderness appears around it?

Responsibility:
What responsibility does this care create?

Protection:
What must be protected?

Neglect:
Where am I failing to care for it?

Conflict:
What other care or value conflicts with it?

Skill:
What skill is needed to care well?

Boundary:
Where does care risk becoming control, anxiety, self-erasure, or obsession?

Meaning:
What meaning does this care give to my life?

Action:
What is the next act of care?

This audit turns care into a structured field.

6.31 Example: Caring About Serious Work

Object of Care:
Serious work.

Form:
Care for craft, contribution, truth, and visible output.

Evidence:
I return to projects, frameworks, writing, and structural thinking.

Attention:
My attention repeatedly returns to how things are built, organized, and expressed.

Emotion:
I feel frustration when work remains vague, unfinished, or unseen.

Responsibility:
I must practice, finish, revise, and expose work to feedback.

Protection:
Time, attention, standards, and completion cycles must be protected.

Neglect:
I may overdesign and delay final output.

Conflict:
Serious work conflicts with comfort, distraction, and fear of exposure.

Skill:
Finishing, editing, publishing, and feedback integration.

Boundary:
Care for serious work must not become self-punishing perfectionism.

Meaning:
This care connects identity, contribution, and future self.

Action:
Complete one bounded work artifact and review it.

This makes care operational.

Freedom.

Care for agency, self-direction, and life design.

Emotion:

Boundary:

Meaning:

Action:

Freedom becomes value through care and practice.

Object of Care:
Love.

Care for relationship, recognition, mutuality, and shared life.

My attention returns to intimacy, trust, communication, and belonging.

Emotion:

Health.

6.35 What I Care About

Care reveals the living structure of value.

It shows what the self cannot treat as nothing.

It appears through attention, attachment, pain, anger, grief, responsibility, protection, repair, time, and commitment.

The mature position is:

I will not judge my values only by what I claim.

I will observe what I care about.

I will notice where attention returns.

I will listen to pain, anger, and grief as possible value signals.

I will distinguish care from anxiety, control, obligation, and possession.

I will let care become responsibility.

I will protect what matters.

I will repair what I have damaged.

I will arrange care into priority.

I will turn care into action.

Care is where value becomes personal.

Value says:

This is important.

Care says:

This matters to me.

Responsibility says:

Because this matters, I must act.

Meaning begins there.

A person does not build a meaningful life by chasing every desire.

They build it by discovering what they truly care about, protecting it with responsibility, and organizing action around it.

That is the work of this chapter.
