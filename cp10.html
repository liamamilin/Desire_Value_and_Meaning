11  Competing Values

A serious life is not difficult only because bad things exist.

It is difficult because good things compete.

Freedom is good.
Security is good.
Achievement is good.
Health is good.
Loyalty is good.
Truth is good.
Ambition is good.
Peace is good.
Love is good.
Independence is good.
Family is good.
Self-direction is good.
Recognition is good.
Privacy is good.
Responsibility is good.
Rest is good.

The problem is that these goods cannot always be maximized together.

A person may want freedom, but freedom often requires uncertainty.

A person may want security, but security often requires constraint.

A person may want achievement, but achievement often consumes energy.

A person may want health, but health often requires limits on achievement.

A person may want loyalty, but loyalty may ask for silence.

A person may want truth, but truth may disturb belonging.

A person may want ambition, but ambition may create pressure.

A person may want peace, but peace may reduce scale.

This chapter begins from a central claim:

Competing values are conflicts between real goods, not merely conflicts between wisdom and weakness.

The immature self asks:

Which side is right?

The mature self asks:

What good does each side protect?

What does each side cost?

Which value should lead here?

Which value must still receive minimum protection?

What sacrifice can I consciously accept?

Competing values require judgment.

Not slogans.

11.1 Value Conflict Is Not Moral Failure

A person may feel ashamed when values conflict.

They may think:

If I were clearer, I would know what to choose.

If I were stronger, I would not feel torn.

If I truly valued freedom, I would not care about security.

If I truly valued truth, I would not fear losing belonging.

If I truly valued ambition, I would not want peace.

If I truly valued love, I would not need independence.

But this is false.

Inner conflict does not always mean weakness.

Often it means the self is aware of several real goods.

A shallow self may choose quickly because it sees only one value.

A deeper self hesitates because it sees more.

The task is not to eliminate conflict.

The task is to structure it.

The self should ask:

What values are competing?

Why does each value matter?

What would happen if one value completely dominated?

What would happen if one value were completely ignored?

What composition is possible?

A mature value system can hold conflict without collapsing.

11.2 Freedom Versus Security

Freedom and security are one of the most common value conflicts.

Freedom wants:

choice
movement
autonomy
self-direction
creative possibility
control over time
room for becoming
escape from unnecessary control

Security wants:

stability
predictability
income
protection
routine
belonging
reduced risk
survival confidence

Both are real goods.

Freedom without security can become reckless.

Security without freedom can become a cage.

The self may feel this conflict when choosing a job, city, relationship, career path, financial plan, education path, or life transition.

One part says:

I need to leave.

I need to choose.

I need to build my own path.

I need space to become.

I cannot stay inside this structure forever.

Another part says:

What if I fail?

What if I lose income?

What if others judge me?

What if I cannot survive the transition?

What if freedom becomes chaos?

The conflict is not between courage and cowardice only.

It is between agency and protection.

11.3 When Freedom Dominates

If freedom dominates without security, life may become unstable.

The self may reject all constraints.

It may leave too quickly.

It may confuse autonomy with escape.

It may despise planning.

It may refuse ordinary responsibilities.

It may break useful structures before building replacements.

Freedom becomes distorted when it says:

Any constraint is oppression.

Any routine is imprisonment.

Any responsibility is control.

Any stable path is cowardice.

Any compromise means I am not free.

This is immature freedom.

Real freedom requires structure.

It needs money, skill, discipline, health, boundary, and judgment.

The self should ask:

Am I pursuing freedom,
or escaping discomfort?

What structure would make this freedom sustainable?

What responsibilities must I accept if I want more agency?

What security is necessary for freedom to survive?

Freedom must be built.

Not merely declared.

11.4 When Security Dominates

If security dominates without freedom, life may become narrow.

The self may avoid risk indefinitely.

It may stay in a job, relationship, city, identity, or role long after it has become too small.

It may call fear realism.

It may call stagnation maturity.

It may trade life direction for predictability.

Security becomes distorted when it says:

Do not risk.

Do not leave.

Do not try.

Do not become different.

Do not disappoint others.

Do not enter uncertainty.

Stay where survival feels guaranteed.

This is not always wisdom.

Sometimes it is fear wearing the language of responsibility.

The self should ask:

How much security do I actually need?

How much security is enough to act?

Where has security become avoidance?

What future am I sacrificing to stay safe?

What would I regret not attempting?

Security should support life.

It should not replace life.

11.5 Composing Freedom and Security

Freedom and security should not simply destroy each other.

They should be composed.

A mature structure may say:

Freedom leads,
but security provides the runway.

Or:

Security leads in this season,
but freedom must receive protected space.

The question becomes:

What is the minimum security needed for responsible freedom?

What is the minimum freedom needed for a life that still feels owned?

Examples:

A person may keep a stable job temporarily while building a serious exit path.

A person may save money before entering a risky transition.

A person may reduce expenses to increase freedom.

A person may choose a less prestigious path because it gives more agency.

A person may accept short-term constraint to build long-term autonomy.

The goal is not pure freedom or pure security.

The goal is a structure where security serves freedom, and freedom does not destroy security irresponsibly.

11.6 Achievement Versus Health

Achievement and health often compete.

Achievement wants:

output
progress
recognition
mastery
completion
competition
visible evidence
future advancement

Health wants:

sleep
recovery
stability
movement
nutrition
emotional regulation
sustainable rhythm
long-term functioning

Both are real goods.

Achievement gives life evidence, confidence, contribution, and movement.

Health gives life capacity, continuity, and embodied stability.

But achievement often pressures the body.

Health often limits the speed of achievement.

The self may say:

I need to work harder.

I need to catch up.

I need to prove myself.

I need to produce evidence.

I cannot rest now.

The body may answer:

I am tired.

I am tense.

I need sleep.

I need recovery.

I cannot carry this pressure forever.

This is not a small conflict.

It is a conflict between building and carrying.

The self wants to build.

The body must carry the building.

11.7 When Achievement Dominates

If achievement dominates without health, the body becomes a resource to exploit.

The self may begin to think:

Sleep later.

Rest later.

Health later.

Relationships later.

Body later.

Everything later.

First, prove.

This may produce short-term output.

But over time, the cost appears.

The person becomes exhausted, anxious, numb, irritable, physically unstable, or unable to feel meaning.

Achievement without health becomes extraction.

The self extracts from the body, but does not replenish it.

The self should ask:

What kind of achievement am I pursuing?

Does this achievement require temporary strain or chronic destruction?

What recovery is built into the system?

Is this ambition value-driven or shame-driven?

Would I still respect this achievement if it destroyed the body that carried it?

Achievement that destroys the conditions of future action is not fully rational.

It may be impressive.

But it is structurally unsound.

11.8 When Health Dominates

If health dominates in a distorted way, it may become avoidance.

The self may use health as a reason to avoid challenge, exposure, discipline, or responsibility.

It may say:

I need rest.
I need peace.
I need recovery.
I cannot push myself.
I should not stress myself.

Sometimes this is true.

Sometimes the body genuinely needs restoration.

But sometimes health language hides fear.

The self should ask:

Is this real recovery,
or avoidance of meaningful difficulty?

Am I protecting the body,
or protecting fear?

Does this rest restore capacity?

Or does it become endless postponement?

What level of effort is healthy but still serious?

Health should not become an excuse for never building.

A healthy life still contains effort.

The question is proportion.

11.9 Composing Achievement and Health

Achievement and health should be designed together.

A mature position says:

I want serious achievement,
but not through systematic self-destruction.

The self asks:

What output rhythm can my body sustain?

What achievement matters enough for temporary strain?

What recovery is non-negotiable?

What signs show that strain has become damage?

What health structure allows long-term achievement?

Examples:

A person may work intensely during a defined sprint, then schedule recovery.

A person may reduce unnecessary perfectionism to protect energy.

A person may build smaller completion cycles instead of chaotic overwork.

A person may define success by durable output, not heroic exhaustion.

A person may treat sleep as part of the achievement system.

Health is not the enemy of achievement.

Health is the infrastructure of achievement.

11.10 Loyalty Versus Truth

Loyalty and truth often compete.

Loyalty wants:

faithfulness
gratitude
protection
belonging
continuity
respect for history
care for people or groups

Truth wants:

accuracy
honesty
reality
evidence
moral clarity
clear speech
refusal of denial

Both are real goods.

A person may be loyal to family, friends, mentors, culture, profession, institution, community, nation, or old self.

But truth may reveal something uncomfortable.

A family pattern is harmful.

A friend acted wrongly.

A mentor was limited.

A culture contains shame.

A profession rewards distortion.

An institution protects image more than reality.

An old identity no longer fits.

The self may feel:

If I speak truth, I betray loyalty.

If I remain silent, I betray reality.

If I criticize what formed me, I am ungrateful.

If I defend it blindly, I become dishonest.

This conflict is difficult because both sides touch belonging.

11.11 When Loyalty Dominates

If loyalty dominates without truth, loyalty becomes denial.

The self may protect people, groups, or old identities from necessary reality.

It may say:

Do not say that.

Do not expose this.

Do not question them.

Do not make conflict.

Do not be ungrateful.

Do not betray where you came from.

This form of loyalty may preserve surface harmony.

But it can also preserve harm.

Loyalty without truth can become complicity.

The self should ask:

What am I protecting?

A real relationship?

Or an illusion?

What truth am I avoiding in the name of loyalty?

Who is harmed by silence?

What would truthful loyalty require?

Loyalty should not mean blindness.

A deeper loyalty may require correction.

11.12 When Truth Dominates

If truth dominates without loyalty, truth can become cold, destructive, or arrogant.

The self may use truth to cut ties, humiliate others, dismiss history, or deny gratitude.

It may say:

I am just telling the truth.

Facts matter more than feelings.

If they cannot handle it, that is their problem.

I owe nothing to the past.

Sometimes hard truth is necessary.

But truth without care can become violence.

The self should ask:

Am I speaking truth to repair reality,
or to punish?

Am I using truth to avoid tenderness?

Can I name harm without erasing gratitude?

Can I be accurate and humane?

What relationship, if any, should truth try to preserve?

Truth is not less true when spoken with care.

Care does not require distortion.

The mature task is truthful loyalty.

11.13 Composing Loyalty and Truth

A mature position says:

I will not use loyalty to hide reality.

I will not use truth to destroy what can be repaired.

I will be loyal to the good,
and truthful about the harm.

This allows complexity.

The self can say:

My family gave me real care,
and also transmitted fear.

My culture gave me discipline,
and also taught shame.

My school gave me opportunity,
and also installed narrow standards.

My old identity protected me,
and also limited me.

This structure avoids crude rejection and blind defense.

The self should ask:

What deserves gratitude?

What requires correction?

What must be named?

What must be protected?

What must be released?

Truth and loyalty mature when they can coexist without denial.

11.14 Ambition Versus Peace

Ambition and peace often compete.

Ambition wants:

growth
expansion
achievement
excellence
influence
creation
recognition
future movement

Peace wants:

calm
rest
simplicity
inner stability
low conflict
freedom from constant pressure
emotional quiet

Both are real goods.

Ambition can give life energy, direction, and scale.

Peace can protect sanity, body, relationship, and inner order.

But ambition can become restless pressure.

Peace can become avoidance.

The self may say:

I want to build something serious.

I want to rise.

I want to create.

I want to become more capable.

I want to make my life count.

Another part says:

I want quiet.

I want to stop proving.

I want to breathe.

I want to live without constant comparison.

I want ordinary peace.

This conflict is not between greatness and weakness.

It is between expansion and rest.

11.15 When Ambition Dominates

If ambition dominates without peace, life becomes pressure.

The self may treat every hour as a test.

Every person as comparison.

Every delay as failure.

Every rest period as guilt.

Every achievement as not enough.

Ambition becomes distorted when it says:

More.

Faster.

Higher.

Prove.

Catch up.

Do not stop.

Do not be ordinary.

Do not disappear.

This ambition may look powerful.

But if shame drives it, it cannot rest.

The self should ask:

What ambition remains if shame is removed?

What do I want to build, not merely prove?

What scale is meaningful?

What cost is acceptable?

What peace must be protected so ambition does not consume life?

Ambition must be purified.

From proving to building.

From panic to direction.

From comparison to contribution.

11.16 When Peace Dominates

If peace dominates without ambition, life may become small.

The self may avoid challenge, exposure, growth, or responsibility in the name of calm.

Peace becomes distorted when it says:

Do not try.

Do not risk.

Do not speak.

Do not want too much.

Do not enter conflict.

Do not build anything that could disturb you.

This is not deep peace.

It is fear of disturbance.

The self should ask:

Is this peace restorative,
or avoidant?

Does this peace make life more alive,
or smaller?

What meaningful ambition am I refusing because it creates discomfort?

What challenge would disturb me but also grow me?

Peace should not become a beautiful name for stagnation.

True peace can support meaningful ambition.

11.17 Composing Ambition and Peace

A mature position says:

I will pursue ambition that builds life,
not ambition that consumes life.

I will protect peace that restores life,
not peace that avoids life.

The self can ask:

What ambition is truly mine?

What peace is truly needed?

What pace allows growth without self-destruction?

What season requires intensity?

What season requires recovery?

What ambition can I pursue without becoming owned by comparison?

Examples:

A person may pursue a serious project with defined work blocks and protected rest.

A person may reduce status competition and focus on meaningful contribution.

A person may choose one major ambition rather than chasing every signal.

A person may accept a slower path to preserve health and relationships.

A person may design peace as recovery, not withdrawal from life.

Ambition and peace can coexist when ambition has standards and peace has courage.

11.18 Love Versus Independence

Love and independence often compete.

Love wants:

closeness
attachment
mutual care
being known
shared life
vulnerability
emotional presence

Independence wants:

autonomy
self-direction
boundary
space
self-possession
freedom from control
personal agency

Both are real goods.

Love without independence can become dependency.

Independence without love can become isolation.

The self may say:

I want to be loved.

I want someone to know me.

I want to share life.

I want closeness.

I want to belong with someone.

Another part says:

I do not want to lose myself.

I do not want to be controlled.

I do not want to depend too much.

I do not want love to swallow my direction.

I need space to remain myself.

This conflict often comes from old attachment wounds or control experiences.

But it also reflects a real structural tension.

Human beings need both connection and selfhood.

11.19 When Love Dominates

If love dominates without independence, the self may disappear into attachment.

It may over-adapt.

It may fear disagreement.

It may confuse being chosen with being safe.

It may accept loss of dignity to preserve closeness.

It may make the other person the center of self-worth.

Love becomes distorted when it says:

Do not lose them at any cost.

Do not have needs that disturb them.

Do not be too different.

Do not protect too much space.

If they leave, you are nothing.

This is not mature love.

It is attachment without enough selfhood.

The self should ask:

Am I loving,
or disappearing?

Am I caring,
or trying to secure my worth through being chosen?

What part of myself am I sacrificing to keep connection?

Can this love include boundary?

Love should expand the self’s reality.

Not erase it.

11.20 When Independence Dominates

If independence dominates without love, the self may become isolated.

It may reject need.

It may avoid vulnerability.

It may treat dependence as humiliation.

It may leave before intimacy deepens.

It may call self-protection freedom.

Independence becomes distorted when it says:

Need no one.

Trust no one.

Do not depend.

Do not be known too deeply.

Do not let anyone matter enough to hurt you.

This may protect the self from pain.

But it also blocks love.

The self should ask:

Am I protecting autonomy,
or avoiding vulnerability?

What kind of dependence is actually healthy?

Can I receive care without losing agency?

Can I let someone matter without surrendering selfhood?

Independence should make love freer.

Not impossible.

11.21 Composing Love and Independence

A mature position says:

I want love that includes selfhood.

I want independence that allows connection.

I want attachment without self-erasure.

I want autonomy without emotional exile.

The self can ask:

What boundaries make love safer?

What vulnerability makes independence less lonely?

What shared life can preserve individual direction?

What dependence is mutual and respectful?

What independence is defensive and excessive?

Love and independence can support each other.

A person with real independence can love without clinging.

A person with real love can be independent without emotional exile.

The task is not to choose only one.

The task is to design relational agency.

11.22 Recognition Versus Privacy

Recognition and privacy often compete.

Recognition wants:

visibility
witness
respect
audience
feedback
being seen accurately
social reality

Privacy wants:

inner space
protection
quiet
freedom from judgment
control over exposure
depth without performance

Both are real goods.

Recognition can make work, effort, pain, and contribution visible.

Privacy protects depth, dignity, and freedom.

The self may say:

I want my work to be seen.

I want accurate recognition.

I want to share what I make.

I want to stop hiding.

Another part says:

I do not want to perform.

I do not want to be judged.

I do not want exposure to control me.

I need inner life protected.

Recognition without privacy can become performance.

Privacy without recognition can become invisibility.

11.23 Composing Recognition and Privacy

A mature position says:

I will seek accurate recognition for real contribution.

I will protect enough privacy to preserve inner depth and freedom.

The self should ask:

What should be visible?

What should remain private?

What audience is appropriate?

What level of exposure serves contribution?

What level of exposure becomes performance?

What privacy protects depth?

What privacy preserves shame?

Examples:

A person may publish finished work while keeping early drafts private.

A person may share ideas with a serious audience rather than chasing mass attention.

A person may maintain personal privacy while making professional contribution visible.

A person may use bounded exposure to build recognition without losing self-possession.

Recognition and privacy require exposure design.

Not total hiding.

Not total display.

11.24 Responsibility Versus Rest

Responsibility and rest often compete.

Responsibility wants:

duty
care
promise-keeping
work completion
family support
repair
reliability
future protection

Rest wants:

recovery
sleep
quiet
bodily restoration
mental space
emotional reset
release from pressure

Both are real goods.

Responsibility keeps life trustworthy.

Rest keeps life sustainable.

The self may say:

I must finish.

I must help.

I must keep the promise.

I must support them.

I must carry this.

Another part says:

I am exhausted.

I need recovery.

I cannot continue like this.

I need to stop.

I need care too.

If responsibility always wins, burnout follows.

If rest always wins, trust and structure may collapse.

11.25 Composing Responsibility and Rest

A mature position says:

I will not abandon responsibility.

I will not destroy myself in the name of responsibility.

I will make rest part of responsible life.

The self should ask:

What responsibility is truly mine?

What is urgent?

What can wait?

What can be delegated?

What is guilt pretending to be responsibility?

What rest is needed for sustainable action?

What rest is recovery,
and what rest is avoidance?

Rest should be designed as infrastructure for responsibility.

Responsibility should be bounded so it does not consume the body.

11.26 Dignity Versus Attachment

Dignity and attachment often compete.

Dignity wants:

self-respect
boundary
non-humiliation
standing upright
refusal of degradation
inner worth not dependent on begging

Attachment wants:

closeness
belonging
not losing the other
being chosen
emotional continuity
relationship security

Both are real goods.

Attachment is human.

Dignity is necessary.

The conflict appears when keeping attachment requires accepting degradation.

The self may say:

I do not want to lose this person.

I do not want to be alone.

I still care.

I still hope it can change.

I need this bond.

Dignity says:

This treatment is not acceptable.

I am disappearing.

I am begging for basic respect.

I am shrinking to keep connection.

This attachment is costing self-respect.

This is one of the most painful value conflicts because choosing dignity may require grief.

11.27 Composing Dignity and Attachment

The first question is repair.

Can the relationship be repaired?

Can respect return?

Can boundary be honored?

Can both attachment and dignity survive?

If yes, the task is repair and boundary.

If no, dignity may need to lead.

A mature position says:

I value attachment,
but I cannot make attachment depend on self-erasure.

I value dignity,
even when dignity requires loss.

The self should ask:

What attachment am I protecting?

What dignity is being violated?

What boundary would test whether repair is possible?

What minimum respect is non-negotiable?

What grief must I accept if dignity leads?

Love without dignity becomes dependency.

Dignity without tenderness can become cold pride.

The mature self protects both where possible, and chooses dignity where necessary.

11.28 Stability Versus Growth

Stability and growth often compete.

Stability wants:

continuity
routine
predictability
safety
preserved structure
emotional calm
known environment

Growth wants:

challenge
change
risk
learning
expansion
new identity
higher capacity
future transformation

Both are real goods.

Stability gives ground.

Growth gives movement.

The self may say:

I need calm.

I need predictable income.

I need routine.

I need stability.

I cannot keep changing everything.

Another part says:

I am becoming stagnant.

I need challenge.

I need a new field.

I need to take a risk.

I cannot live the same life forever.

Stability without growth becomes stagnation.

Growth without stability becomes chaos.

11.29 Composing Stability and Growth

A mature position says:

I will build enough stability to support growth.

I will not use stability to avoid becoming.

The self should ask:

What stability is necessary now?

What stability has become a cage?

What growth is meaningful?

What growth is reckless?

What support does this growth require?

What old structure must be kept temporarily?

What old structure must be released?

Examples:

A person may keep stable routines while entering a new field.

A person may maintain financial stability while testing a new path.

A person may grow through small experiments instead of total rupture.

A person may create a stable environment so the nervous system can tolerate growth.

Growth needs ground.

Stability needs direction.

11.30 The Structure of Competing Values

Every competing-value conflict can be mapped.

Value A:
What good does it protect?

Value B:
What good does it protect?

Conflict:
Where do they oppose each other?

Distortion A:
What happens if Value A dominates excessively?

Distortion B:
What happens if Value B dominates excessively?

Season:
Which value should lead now?

Minimum:
What minimum protection does the secondary value need?

Sacrifice:
What must be given up?

Composition:
How can both values be honored in a realistic structure?

Decision:
What action follows?

This map prevents crude choices.

It allows the self to see the conflict as architecture.

Not chaos.

11.31 Example: Freedom Versus Security Map

Value A:
Freedom.

Good Protected:
Agency, self-direction, becoming, control over time.

Value B:
Security.

Good Protected:
Survival, stability, income, reduced risk.

Conflict:
Freedom requires uncertainty. Security prefers predictability.

Distortion A:
Freedom becomes reckless escape.

Distortion B:
Security becomes fear-based stagnation.

Season:
If the self is in transition, freedom may need to lead.

Minimum:
Security needs a financial runway and risk plan.

Sacrifice:
Some comfort and predictability must be reduced.

Composition:
Build savings, skill, and transition structure before major exit.

Decision:
Move toward freedom through staged security-backed action.

This turns conflict into design.

11.32 Example: Achievement Versus Health Map

Value A:
Achievement.

Good Protected:
Progress, mastery, output, recognition, future evidence.

Value B:
Health.

Good Protected:
Body, recovery, sustainability, long-term functioning.

Conflict:
Achievement demands effort. Health demands recovery.

Distortion A:
Achievement becomes self-extraction.

Distortion B:
Health becomes avoidance of challenge.

Season:
If the body is strained, health may need to lead temporarily.

Minimum:
Achievement still needs small completion cycles.

Sacrifice:
Some speed must be sacrificed.

Composition:
Build a sustainable output rhythm with recovery.

Decision:
Pursue serious work without chronic bodily destruction.

This avoids both burnout and avoidance.

11.33 Example: Truth Versus Belonging Map

Value A:
Truth.

Good Protected:
Reality, honesty, clear understanding, integrity.

Value B:
Belonging.

Good Protected:
Relationship, acceptance, continuity, social safety.

Conflict:
Truth may disturb belonging. Belonging may pressure silence.

Distortion A:
Truth becomes harshness or isolation.

Distortion B:
Belonging becomes self-deception.

Season:
If falsehood is damaging the self, truth may need to lead.

Minimum:
Belonging deserves care in timing and language.

Sacrifice:
Some comfort or approval may be lost.

Composition:
Speak necessary truth with care and proportion.

Decision:
Seek belonging that can survive reality.

This gives truth and belonging mature form.

11.34 Example: Ambition Versus Peace Map

Value A:
Ambition.

Good Protected:
Growth, creation, achievement, future expansion.

Value B:
Peace.

Good Protected:
Calm, recovery, inner stability, freedom from constant pressure.

Conflict:
Ambition creates tension. Peace limits intensity.

Distortion A:
Ambition becomes endless proving.

Distortion B:
Peace becomes avoidance.

Season:
If life is stagnant, ambition may need to lead.
If the body is depleted, peace may need to lead.

Minimum:
The non-leading value must still be protected.

Sacrifice:
Either scale or comfort must be reduced.

Composition:
Pursue one meaningful ambition with protected recovery.

Decision:
Build without becoming owned by pressure.

This turns ambition and peace into rhythm.

11.35 Competing Values Require Adult Judgment

Childish value thinking says:

One value must be completely right.

The other must be completely wrong.

Adult value thinking says:

Both may be good.

Both may be distorted.

Both may need limits.

One may need to lead now.

The other may need minimum protection.

The choice will have cost.

This is the essence of value maturity.

A person does not become mature by eliminating conflict.

They become mature by making conscious trade-offs.

The self should ask:

What good am I protecting?

What good am I sacrificing?

Can I accept this sacrifice?

What regret am I willing to carry?

What self-respect will this choice preserve?

What must I review later?

Competing values require courage because there is often no painless choice.

11.36 Competing Values

Competing values are not signs that the self lacks values.

They are signs that the self has multiple goods to protect.

The mature position is:

I will not simplify every conflict into right versus wrong.

I will identify the good on each side.

I will examine the distortion of each side.

I will decide which value leads in this season.

I will protect minimums where possible.

I will accept that real choice involves sacrifice.

I will design compositions, not only oppositions.

I will let conflict clarify my value hierarchy.

A serious life is shaped not only by what a person values, but by how they choose when values collide.

Freedom must meet security.

Achievement must meet health.

Loyalty must meet truth.

Ambition must meet peace.

Love must meet independence.

Recognition must meet privacy.

Responsibility must meet rest.

Dignity must meet attachment.

Stability must meet growth.

The self becomes more coherent when it can hold these conflicts consciously.

Not without pain.

But without blindness.

That is the work of this chapter.
